How active time is estimated

GitHub does not record when a PR is being worked on versus waiting in review. We infer it from the timing of each PR's events (commits, reviews, comments, push events). A concrete walk-through:

Alice pushes a commit to PR #4 at 8:00 AM Tuesday. She pushes another to the same PR at 8:55 AM, then a third at 9:30 AM. All three events are within 4.0 hours of each other, so they form a single work session on PR #4 — from 8:00 to 9:30 AM, 1.5 hours of active time credited to that PR.

At 9:35 AM Alice switches to PR #3, pushes a commit, and works on it through the rest of the morning. Because more than 4.0 hours later Alice has not touched PR #4 again, PR #4's work session is considered closed at 9:30 AM. The 4-plus hours of clock time between 9:30 AM and her next event on PR #4 count as wait time for PR #4, not active time — even though Alice was working, she was working on something else. (If a reviewer comments on PR #4 in that gap, that counts as a separate work session for PR #4.)

A lone isolated event (e.g. one comment with no surrounding activity) would otherwise count for zero seconds of active time. We credit it with at least 30.0 minutes so a short review pass isn't recorded as having taken zero work.

Both numbers are tunable: --gap-hours and --min-cluster-minutes. The defaults (4.0h / 30.0 min) are reasonable for solo-author-plus-async-review patterns; teams that pair-program or swarm a PR may want a higher gap; teams with very frequent commits may want a lower one. Raising the gap raises FE; lowering it lowers FE.

Caveats

  • Do not compare this number across engineers — wait time on a single PR is shared between author, reviewer, and CI, and one slow reviewer can drop an author's per-PR FE to single digits regardless of how fast the author actually worked.
  • Active time uses gap=4.0h and min_cluster=30.0min — raising the gap credits longer idle stretches as active, which raises FE; lowering it does the opposite.

Vocabulary

Cycle time
Wall-clock time from when a PR was opened until it was merged. The clock starts at `created_at` and stops at `merged_at`.
Active time
The share of cycle time covered by clusters of activity events (commits, reviews, comments). Events more than `gap-hours` apart form separate clusters; each cluster credits at least `min-cluster-minutes` of active time.
Wait time
cycle_time − active_time. Time the PR spent waiting in queues (awaiting review, blocked, etc.). This is where time leaks out of the system — long wait time on a few PRs drives the portfolio FE down more than slow individual work does.
Flow efficiency
active_time / cycle_time. Reported per-PR and as a portfolio.
Portfolio flow efficiency
Σ active ÷ Σ cycle across every merged PR in the window. Because totals are summed before dividing, long-running PRs dominate the number — exactly when you want them to. Contrast with mean(per-PR FE), which weights every PR equally: fifty trivial 5-minute PRs at 100% would drown out one 30-day PR at 5%, even though that 30-day PR is where your wait time actually lives.
Mean per-PR FE (and why not to act on it)
Simple average of each PR's individual flow efficiency. It tells you what a typical PR's ratio looks like in isolation, but it does not reflect the system — a long tail of small fast PRs makes it look great even when one big PR sits in review for weeks. Reported for transparency; do not optimize against it. Use Portfolio FE instead.
